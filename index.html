The Core Philosophy

How to think about health and longevity before doing anything else

3 min read
The Guiding Principle

Train for the person you need to be at 80 — not the person you want to be at 35.

Most people think about fitness in terms of how they look or perform right now. Longevity thinking runs in the opposite direction. You start with the end in mind: what do you need to be able to do at 80 or 90 to have a good life? Then you work backwards from there.

This is called backcasting — and it changes everything about how you train, what you eat, and what you prioritize. The goal isn't to be the fastest or the most jacked at 40. The goal is to be functional, strong, and metabolically healthy for as long as possible.

The body fails before the mind for most people. Physical frailty — not cancer, not heart disease — is what quietly ends quality of life first. The good news: it's almost entirely preventable with the right habits, started at any age.

  • It is never too late to start — but the longer you wait, the harder you have to work to catch up
  • The tools available today are the most powerful longevity interventions that exist — use them now
  • The goal is compression of morbidity — being healthy until very close to the end, not just living longer
  • Consistency over years matters more than any individual training session or perfect diet week
  • Don't wait for a miracle drug or breakthrough — the evidence for exercise, protein, and sleep is already overwhelming

The Four Pillars of Longevity

Everything that matters for long-term physical health fits into four categories

4 min read

There are four things you need to train if you want to be physically capable for life. Miss any one of them and the others eventually break down. Think of it as a portfolio — not a single investment.

🧘
01

Stability

Balance, coordination, and the ability to control force safely through a full range of motion. The foundation everything else is built on. Most overlooked. Most important for injury prevention.

💪
02

Strength

Muscle mass, bone density, and the ability to produce force. The single best insurance policy against aging. Non-negotiable at any age, any sex, any fitness level.

💚
03

Aerobic Base

Zone 2 training — low-intensity cardio that builds mitochondrial health, fat oxidation, and metabolic efficiency. The engine that keeps everything else running.

🔥
04

Peak Output

Zone 5 / VO2 max work — brief, hard efforts that maintain peak cardiovascular capacity. Even one session per week makes a measurable difference to longevity.

Start With One Thing

If you're just starting out, don't try to do all four at once. Pick one — preferably a 20-minute walk daily — and build from there over 90 days. Consistency beats optimization every time.

Life Happens in Zone 1, 2, and 5

Most of daily life — walking, climbing stairs, carrying groceries, playing with kids — happens at low intensity. Brief moments of high effort happen occasionally. Training these two extremes prepares you for real life.

Performance vs. Longevity

Training to run a sub-2:40 marathon or deadlift 3x bodyweight is not co-linear with longevity — it may actually cost you at the margin. The goal is optimal health span, not peak athletic performance.

The Retirement Savings Analogy

Starting to exercise at 50 is like starting to save at 45. Better than never, but you'll need to work harder to catch up. The compound interest of consistent training over decades is enormous.

Cardio & Metabolism

Zone 2 Training

The most underrated tool in health and longevity — explained simply

6 min read
What Is Zone 2?

The pace where you can hold a conversation — but just barely.

Zone 2 is low-intensity aerobic exercise performed at the upper limit of your ability to burn fat efficiently. It's not a casual stroll — it's a purposeful, sustained effort where your breathing is elevated but you could speak in full sentences if you had to. Think brisk walking uphill, easy cycling, light jogging, or rowing at a steady pace.

At this intensity, your body's primary fuel is fat. Your mitochondria — the tiny power plants inside your muscle cells — are doing most of the work. The more you train in Zone 2, the better your mitochondria get at their job. And mitochondrial health is one of the strongest determinants of how you age.

How to Know You're in Zone 2

The simplest test: you can speak in full sentences, but you wouldn't want to. You're breathing noticeably harder than at rest. If you can sing, you're too easy. If you can't talk at all, you're too hard. This is the "talk test."

Why Fat Burning Matters

The ability to burn fat efficiently at rest and during exercise is one of the best markers of metabolic health. Sedentary people lose this ability over time. Zone 2 training restores and improves it. Better fat oxidation = better energy, better body composition, lower disease risk.

Lactate Is Not the Enemy

You've heard that "lactic acid" causes soreness and fatigue. That's outdated. Lactate is actually a fuel — your body shuttles it between cells and burns it in your mitochondria. The ability to clear lactate quickly is a key marker of fitness. Zone 2 trains exactly this.

Mitochondrial Biogenesis

Zone 2 training signals your cells to build more mitochondria and improve the ones you already have. More mitochondria = more capacity to produce energy aerobically. This adaptation takes weeks to months — you won't feel it immediately, but it compounds over time.

Zone 2 vs Zone 5 — Two Different Goals

Zone 2 builds your aerobic base and metabolic efficiency — this is volume work, done frequently. Zone 5 (near-maximal effort) maintains peak cardiovascular capacity — this is done briefly, once a week. They complement each other. Don't confuse them.

Elite Athletes as the Proof of Concept

World-class endurance athletes spend 80% of their training at Zone 2 intensity — not grinding hard every day. Their exceptional lactate clearance and fat oxidation are the result of years of accumulated low-intensity volume. The principle scales down to everyday people perfectly.

CPT1 and CPT2 — The Fat Transport System

Two proteins (carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1 and 2) are responsible for transporting fat into the mitochondria where it gets burned. In sedentary and metabolically unhealthy individuals, these transporters are significantly downregulated — fat literally can't get in. Zone 2 upregulates them.

Metabolomics — The Future of Fitness Testing

Emerging research can analyze hundreds of metabolites in blood samples to predict metabolic health and training response. Elite cyclists like Pogačar show dramatically different metabolite profiles at equivalent workloads — the science of individual optimization is coming.

VO2 Max & Longevity

The single most powerful predictor of how long you'll live

5 min read

VO2 max is the maximum amount of oxygen your body can use during intense exercise. Think of it as the size of your engine. And it turns out, the size of your engine is one of the strongest predictors of how long you live — and how well.

~50%
reduction in all-cause mortality risk by moving from "low" fitness to just "below average" — the biggest single gain available
5×
lower mortality risk comparing the highest VO2 max quartile to the lowest — stronger than most medications
~10%
decline in VO2 max per decade after age 30 in untrained individuals — training cuts this dramatically
You Don't Need Elite Fitness

The biggest longevity gains come from getting off the floor — moving from sedentary to "a little active." You don't need to be an athlete. You need to not be sedentary. That gap is enormous and closing it changes everything.

Walking Counts

30–40 minutes of brisk walking per day produces measurable cardiovascular benefit. Group walking, walking meetings, or a consistent lunchtime walk are all legitimate starting points. Any movement beats none.

VO2 Max vs. Other Risk Factors

Low cardiorespiratory fitness is a stronger predictor of early death than smoking, diabetes, or high blood pressure in some studies. If you only had time for one health intervention, improving your aerobic fitness would be the most impactful choice.

How Exercise Protects the Heart

Exercise improves endothelial function (the lining of blood vessels), lowers resting blood pressure, improves autonomic nervous system balance, reduces insulin resistance, and decreases chronic inflammation — all simultaneously. No medication does all five.

Heart Rate Variability — A Window Into Health

HRV measures the variation between heartbeats. Higher HRV means your heart is adaptable and your nervous system is balanced. Fit people have significantly higher HRV. It's one of the most practical indicators of recovery readiness and overall cardiovascular health.

How Much Cardio for Longevity?

150 minutes of moderate intensity per week OR 75 minutes vigorous is the minimum meaningful dose. Beyond that, returns continue but diminish. Extreme endurance volumes (very high marathon mileage) may carry some cardiac remodeling risk — still better than sedentary, but not the goal for most people.

Skeletal Muscle Blood Flow

During exercise, blood flow to working muscles can increase up to 100x. Training improves how efficiently this is regulated — muscles get oxygen faster and waste is cleared faster. This is partly why trained athletes recover faster between sets and between sessions.

Autonomic Nervous System Adaptation

Regular aerobic training shifts your autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance at rest — your body becomes more efficient at recovery. This is reflected in a lower resting heart rate, higher HRV, and better stress resilience. It's one of the most underappreciated adaptations to exercise.

Mitochondrial Health

Why the tiny power plants in your cells determine how you age

5 min read

Mitochondria are the structures inside your cells that produce energy. Every movement you make, every thought you have, every heartbeat — all of it runs on energy produced by your mitochondria. Their health is central to virtually every aspect of aging.

What Mitochondria Actually Do

Mitochondria convert oxygen and fuel (fat or glucose) into usable energy (ATP). The more mitochondria you have and the better they work, the more energy you can produce — and the healthier you are. Exercise builds more mitochondria. Inactivity degrades them.

Metabolic Disease Starts Here

In people with type 2 diabetes or obesity, mitochondria don't work properly — they can't burn fat or glucose efficiently. This isn't just a symptom; it's a central cause. And the disregulation appears 10–15 years before any clinical diagnosis. Exercise is the most powerful intervention to reverse it.

The Glucose-Lactate Fork

When glucose enters a cell, it becomes pyruvate. Pyruvate then faces a choice: enter the mitochondria and be burned for energy (good), or get converted to lactate (less efficient). The healthier your mitochondria, the more pyruvate goes the productive route. Zone 2 training shifts this balance.

The Athletes Paradox

Both elite athletes and people with type 2 diabetes accumulate fat droplets inside muscle cells. But in athletes, this fat is constantly cycled and burned — it's active, not stagnant. In diabetics, it accumulates and releases inflammatory signals. Same molecule, opposite metabolic fate.

Ceramides and Cardiovascular Disease

Stagnant intramuscular fat is high in ceramides — molecules linked to atherosclerosis and cardiovascular disease. This may explain why ~80% of people with type 2 diabetes also have cardiovascular disease. The shared root cause appears to be mitochondrial dysfunction.

Mitochondrial Dysfunction Precedes Symptoms

Measurable disregulation in glucose and fat metabolism appears in sedentary individuals up to 15 years before any clinical symptoms or diagnosis. This is both alarming and empowering — there's a large window where intervention works, long before anything shows on a standard blood test.

Strength & Muscle

Strength for Longevity

Why resistance training is the single best anti-aging intervention available

6 min read
The Bottom Line

There is nobody who shouldn't be lifting weights. Nobody.

Not women. Not older adults. Not people with injuries. Not beginners. The only exception is someone who has decided they don't want to live the longest, healthiest life possible. If that's not you — lift weights.

Muscle mass is the most powerful predictor of functional independence in old age. It protects against falls, fractures, metabolic disease, and poor surgical outcomes. It builds bone. It controls blood glucose. It keeps you out of a wheelchair.

Grip Strength Predicts Mortality

Grip strength — how hard you can squeeze — is one of the best single predictors of all-cause mortality. It's a proxy for total muscle mass, connective tissue health, and neuromuscular function. If your grip is weak, your whole system tends to be weak.

Falls Are Catastrophic After 65

A hip fracture in an 80-year-old has a one-year mortality rate comparable to many cancers. Falls don't just cause injuries — they trigger a cascade of decline. Strength training and balance work are the primary prevention tools. Nothing else comes close.

Catching Yourself Requires Power

When you trip and stumble, the ability to catch yourself before hitting the ground requires explosive power — force produced very quickly. Power declines faster than strength with age. Training it specifically (jumps, medicine ball work, explosive lifts) is essential, not optional.

Bone Density — Use It or Lose It

Bone responds to mechanical stress by getting denser and stronger. Heavy resistance training is the most powerful stimulus for bone building. The LIFT MORE trial showed postmenopausal women with low bone density — training 5×5 at 85%+ of their max twice per week — gained nearly 3% bone density in 8 months. The control group lost over 1%.

Muscle Controls Blood Glucose

75–80% of glucose storage and utilization happens in skeletal muscle. When your muscles are strong and active, they act as a sponge for blood sugar — pulling it out of circulation efficiently. Weak, inactive muscle leads to chronically elevated blood glucose and eventually type 2 diabetes.

Progressive Overload — The Non-Negotiable Law

The body adapts to the stress you put on it. If you never challenge it beyond what it's used to, it doesn't change. Progressive overload means gradually increasing the demand over time — more weight, more reps, better technique, less rest. Without it, you're maintaining at best.

Estrogen and Bone — The Mechanism

When bone experiences mechanical load, that physical stress gets converted into a cellular signal — and estrogen is the molecular messenger that relays it to bone-building cells. After menopause, declining estrogen weakens this relay, accelerating bone loss. Resistance training is the most effective way to reinforce what remains of the signaling pathway.

Aerobic Fitness Before Strength Training

Research shows 6 weeks of steady-state aerobic training before a hypertrophy block produced more muscle growth than going straight into lifting. A better aerobic base improves work capacity, recovery between sets, and the hormonal response to training — even when the primary goal is muscle.

Strength & Muscle

How Muscle Actually Works

The science behind muscle — explained without the jargon

5 min read
Muscle Is an Organ, Not Just Tissue

Muscle doesn't just move your body. It pumps blood back to your heart, stores amino acids for immune function, regulates blood glucose, and sends chemical signals to your brain, liver, and lungs. Every time you contract a muscle, you're doing far more than just moving a limb.

Slow-Twitch vs Fast-Twitch

Slow-twitch muscle fibers are built for endurance — they don't fatigue easily and are used for everyday movement. Fast-twitch fibers produce more force and speed but tire quickly. Both matter. Fast-twitch fibers decline fastest with age — they need to be specifically trained with heavy lifting and explosive work.

Muscle Memory Is Real

Muscle fibers contain multiple cell nuclei — more than any other tissue in the body. When you train, you add nuclei. These persist even when you stop training. This is the biological basis of muscle memory: returning to training after a break is faster because the infrastructure is already there.

You Don't Grow in the Gym

Training is the stimulus. Growth happens during recovery — primarily during sleep, when growth hormone peaks and protein synthesis runs. This is why sleep and protein are not optional extras. The training session is the trigger; the hours afterward are where the adaptation actually occurs.

Soreness Is Not the Goal

Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) is an indicator of novelty — your body encountered something unfamiliar. It is not a reliable marker of a good workout. You can have an excellent, productive training session with no soreness at all. Don't chase pain — chase progression.

The Eccentric Phase — Most Neglected

The lowering phase of any lift (eccentric) produces more force than the lifting phase and creates more stimulus for growth. Most people rush through it. A 2–3 second controlled descent on every rep doubles the productive time under tension compared to dropping the weight.

Myokines — Muscle's Chemical Messages

Contracting muscle releases myokines — signaling molecules that communicate with the brain, liver, bone, and fat tissue. Exercise-released myokines reduce inflammation, improve brain health, stimulate fat burning, and support immune function. This is why the benefits of exercise extend far beyond the muscles being trained.

Movement Quality Before Load

The correct progression: can you do the movement at all → bodyweight only → controlled eccentric → unilateral (single limb) → loaded → loaded and fast → under fatigue. Most people jump straight to load. Adding weight on top of dysfunctional movement patterns is a reliable path to injury.

Aging & Muscle Loss

What happens after 50 — and how to fight back

5 min read

The body changes after 50. These changes are real — but most of them are not inevitable. Understanding what's happening gives you the ability to do something about it.

Muscle Loss Accelerates With Age

Untrained individuals lose 3–8% of muscle mass per decade after 35. The rate accelerates after 60. By 80, a sedentary person may have lost 30–40% of the muscle they had at their peak. This is largely preventable. Resistance training is the intervention. There is no substitute.

Strength Declines Noticeably Around 65

Vestibular changes (balance and spatial awareness) also begin around this age, directly increasing fall risk. This is when the investment in training you made in your 40s and 50s pays off — and when the cost of not having trained becomes very clear.

It Is Never Too Late to Start

Studies consistently show meaningful muscle and strength gains in people in their 70s, 80s, and even 90s with resistance training. The body retains its ability to adapt at any age. The process is slower, but the returns on health and function are arguably larger than they are for a 25-year-old.

Anabolic Resistance — Why Older Adults Need More Protein

With age, the muscle-building machinery grows blunted — it takes more protein and more training stimulus to drive the same response that came easily at 25. A 65-year-old may only get a partial anabolic response from the same protein dose that fully activates synthesis in a younger person. The answer is to increase input, not reduce it.

Tissue Tolerance — The Most Overlooked Concept

Connective tissue (tendons and ligaments) adapts slower than muscle — it lags by weeks to months. This is why the classic torn Achilles happens to the athlete returning from inactivity who goes straight back to full intensity. Tendons need gradual progressive exposure to load and speed.

How to Start at 50+

Step 1: move daily — even a 15-minute walk after dinner. Step 2: add bodyweight resistance work. Step 3: progress to loaded movements with a coach or structured program. Step 4: add Zone 2 cardio. Do not get injured — injury is the enemy of consistency, and consistency is everything.

Bone Density Is Nonlinear in Decline

Bone density doesn't erode at a steady pace — the rate of loss compounds with each decade. The LIFTMOR trial demonstrated that heavy resistance training (5×5 at 85%+ of 1RM) in postmenopausal women with low bone mass produced approximately 3% gains in lumbar spine density over 8 months, while an untrained control group lost over 1% in the same timeframe.

Run a Little. Sprint Occasionally.

Even modest running volume — a few miles per week — preserves the structural resilience of tendons and connective tissue through the lower body, lowering injury risk across all other activities. Adding brief sprinting efforts (such as alternating hard efforts with walking recovery for a few laps) keeps fast-twitch fibers engaged and the surrounding tissue conditioned for load.

Nutrition

Protein — The Priority

The most important nutritional variable for health, muscle, and aging

6 min read
The Muscle-Centric View of Nutrition

Build your diet around two tissues: skeletal muscle and the brain. Everything else adapts.

Protein is not a supplement. It is the primary structural material of the human body. Muscle is where you burn glucose, store amino acids for immune function, and regulate the signals that keep you metabolically healthy. Every meal that lacks sufficient protein is a missed opportunity to maintain and build that system.

The RDA for protein (0.8g per kg bodyweight) is a minimum to prevent deficiency. It is not a target for health, performance, or aging well. The evidence-based target for active people is 1.6–2.2g per kg — and older adults need to trend toward the higher end due to anabolic resistance.

The Simple Target

Aim for 0.7–1g of protein per pound of bodyweight per day. A 180 lb person needs 125–180g of protein daily. This sounds like a lot because it is — most people eat nowhere near this. Start tracking for a week and you'll likely find you're significantly under.

Protein at Every Meal

Spreading protein across 3–4 meals is more effective than loading it all into one meal. Each meal should contain at least 30–40g of quality protein — enough to trigger the muscle-building response. Breakfast is the most commonly neglected meal. Starting the day with a protein anchor sets the tone.

Protein Keeps You Full

Protein is the most satiating macronutrient. High-protein meals reduce hunger for hours afterward, making it significantly easier to eat less overall without white-knuckling hunger. This is one reason high-protein diets consistently outperform low-protein diets for fat loss in research.

Leucine — The Muscle Building Trigger

Leucine is the amino acid that directly triggers muscle protein synthesis via a pathway called mTOR. Think of it as the "on switch." Every meal needs at least 2.5–3g of leucine to fully activate the response. This is why protein quality per meal matters — not just daily totals.

Animal vs. Plant Protein

Animal proteins (meat, eggs, dairy) are complete — they contain all essential amino acids in the right ratios and are high in leucine. Plant proteins are often incomplete and lower in leucine. A healthy plant-based diet is achievable but requires more volume, more knowledge, and often supplementation to hit optimal protein targets.

Protein During a Fat Loss Phase

When eating in a caloric deficit, protein intake should go up, not stay the same. High protein during a cut is the primary lever for preserving muscle mass. Cutting protein to cut calories is one of the most counterproductive things you can do — you'll lose muscle alongside fat.

The Thermic Effect of Protein

Approximately 25–30% of protein's calories are burned just through the process of digesting it — far higher than carbohydrates (5–10%) or fat (2–3%). A 200-calorie protein serving effectively delivers 140–150 net calories. This metabolic advantage accumulates significantly over time.

Eggs — The Evidence Is Clear

Dietary cholesterol from eggs does not meaningfully raise cardiovascular risk in healthy individuals. Decades of research funding the egg-disease link were confounded by context — eggs eaten as part of a fast food meal vs. eggs eaten in a quality diet told completely opposite stories when properly analyzed.

Energy Balance

Calories are real. The math is messier than most people think.

4 min read
Calories Are Just a Unit of Energy

A calorie is simply a measure of how much energy a given food contains. Think of it as a unit — the same way miles measure distance or kilograms measure mass. Claims that calories are fictional ignore basic physics. Energy balance is real. The nuance lies in the details, not in disputing the concept itself.

Why Tracking Isn't Perfect

Calorie counts on food labels have error margins. Your body's calorie expenditure fluctuates. Fiber calories are variable. None of this means calories don't matter — it means the numbers are estimates. Use them as a guide, not gospel. Trends matter more than daily precision.

Sustainable Deficits Win

A modest caloric deficit of 300–500 calories per day produces slow, steady fat loss with minimal muscle loss and minimal metabolic adaptation. Aggressive cuts produce faster short-term results but trigger greater metabolic slowdown, greater muscle loss, and are much harder to sustain.

Metabolic Adaptation Is Real

When you eat less, your body fights back by reducing expenditure — a process called metabolic adaptation. Your resting metabolic rate decreases, you move less unconsciously, and hormones shift to promote fat storage. This is why fat loss always slows over time and why diet breaks have legitimate science behind them.

Body Recomposition — Who Can Do It

Building muscle and losing fat simultaneously is achievable — but primarily for beginners, people returning from a training break, and people with higher body fat percentages. Advanced, lean athletes cannot do this efficiently. For them, gaining and cutting phases are more effective.

Fat Is the Efficient Storage Molecule

The body stores excess energy as fat because fat is anhydrous — it doesn't require water. Glycogen (carbohydrate stored in muscle and liver) requires ~3–4g of water per gram. If you stored all your body's energy as glycogen instead of fat, you would weigh several hundred pounds more. Fat storage is an elegant evolutionary solution.

The Scale Is a Lagging Indicator

Water retention, glycogen stores, hormonal fluctuations, and digestive content all create noise on the scale that can mask real fat loss or gain for days or weeks at a time. Weekly averages, waist measurements, and progress photos provide more signal than daily weigh-ins.

Supplements — What Actually Works

A short list of things worth taking — and a long list of things that aren't

4 min read

The supplement industry is built on weak evidence, aggressive marketing, and the human desire for shortcuts. The list of supplements with genuine, robust evidence is very short. Here it is.

Creatine Monohydrate ✓

The most studied performance supplement in existence. Increases strength, power output, muscle mass, and has emerging evidence for cognitive benefits. 3–5g per day. Timing doesn't matter much. No need for loading phases or expensive forms — plain monohydrate is the gold standard.

Vitamin D ✓

Most people in modern sedentary, indoor lifestyles are deficient. Vitamin D affects immune function, bone health, mood, and muscle function. A blood test will tell you your level — most people need 2,000–5,000 IU daily to maintain optimal range. Inexpensive and well-tolerated.

Omega-3 Fish Oil ✓

EPA and DHA (the active forms in fish oil) have cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory benefits. Most people's diets are significantly imbalanced toward omega-6 fats. 2–3g of combined EPA/DHA per day is a reasonable target. Look for triglyceride form for better absorption.

Magnesium ✓

Involved in hundreds of enzymatic reactions. Most people are deficient due to poor dietary variety and soil depletion. Supplementation improves sleep quality, muscle function, and stress response. Magnesium glycinate or threonate are the best-tolerated forms for sleep and cognition.

Caffeine ✓ (with caveats)

Genuine ergogenic effect — improves strength output, endurance, and mental focus. 3–6mg per kg bodyweight before training. The downside: tolerance develops, it can disrupt sleep if taken too late, and cutting it causes withdrawal. Use strategically, not habitually if possible.

Citrulline Malate ✓

More effective than arginine for increasing nitric oxide (blood flow). Meta-analyses show approximately 5% improvement in power output — meaningful for athletic performance. 6–8g pre-workout. More useful for performance than hypertrophy, though better performance can drive better training.

Protein Powder ✓ (when needed)

Not magic — just food in powder form. Useful when hitting protein targets from whole food alone is impractical. Whey is fast-digesting and high in leucine. Casein is slower-digesting and better suited for overnight. No meaningful difference from food protein if total daily intake is adequate.

Most Everything Else — Skepticism Warranted

BCAAs (redundant if protein intake is sufficient), testosterone boosters, fat burners, collagen supplements for muscle, proprietary pre-workout blends — the evidence for most is thin to nonexistent. The supplement industry profits from complexity. The effective list is simple and boring.

Longevity Metrics

Key Health Markers

What to actually track — and what the numbers mean

4 min read
VO2 Max — Get Tested

Your VO2 max score places you in a percentile for your age and sex. Moving from the bottom 25% to even the next quartile dramatically cuts mortality risk. You don't need to be elite — you need to not be at the bottom. Most gyms and sports medicine clinics offer this test.

Blood Glucose — The Number That Matters Most

Average blood glucose (tracked by HbA1c or continuous glucose monitor) correlates with all-cause mortality across the full range — not just in diabetics. Lower is better, even in the "normal" range. The target is a fasting glucose under 90 mg/dL and HbA1c under 5.4%.

ApoB — Not LDL

ApoB counts the number of atherogenic (artery-clogging) particles in your blood directly. Two people can have identical LDL cholesterol but very different apoB. Standard lipid panels can miss the real risk. Ask your doctor for an apoB test. Optimal is below 60 mg/dL; aggressive prevention targets 30–40 mg/dL.

Blood Pressure — Never Just "Normal"

Even mild, sustained hypertension causes cumulative damage to arterial walls over years. The goal is not just "not hypertensive" — it's optimal, which is closer to 110/70 than 130/80. Exercise, sleep, sodium management, and stress reduction are first-line interventions before medication.

Grip Strength — The Simplest Functional Test

A hand dynamometer test costs almost nothing and takes 30 seconds. Low grip strength correlates with higher all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, and functional decline. It's a crude but powerful proxy for total musculoskeletal health. Track it annually and trend it upward.

CGM — Continuous Glucose Monitoring

Wearing a CGM for 2–4 weeks reveals how specific foods, sleep quality, stress, and exercise affect your individual blood glucose response. Two metrics matter most: average glucose (target under 100 mg/dL) and standard deviation (lower is better — you want stability, not spikes and crashes).

  • Sleep is not optional — it is when growth hormone releases, protein synthesizes, and the brain clears waste
  • Consistency over years beats optimization of any single week — show up, don't get hurt, repeat

Zone 2 vs. Zone 5 — When to Use Each

Zone 2 — Your Aerobic Base

  • Conversational pace — just barely
  • Blood lactate stays stable (~2 mmol/L)
  • 45–60 minutes, 3–4× per week
  • Builds mitochondria and fat oxidation
  • Walking, cycling, easy rowing or jogging
  • The bulk of your cardio training

Zone 5 — Peak Output Work

  • Near-maximal effort — cannot speak
  • Lactate rises sharply
  • Brief intervals — 30 sec to 4 min
  • Maintains VO2 max, peak cardiac output
  • Once per week is enough for longevity
  • Cannot be substituted by more Zone 2
